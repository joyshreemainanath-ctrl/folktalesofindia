Myths and Legends

Garuda's Quest for Acquiring Amrit

Reading time: 5 to 6 min

Cycle: Garuda (The King of Birds)Source: Tale of Astika - Adi Parva, MahabharataTheme: Trial, Battle, Divine Covenant

Shaking the heavens, defeating the army of Devas, and forming a divine covenant with Lord Vishnu.

After satiating his hunger, Garuda set out on a quest to obtain the divine Amrit (elixir) to free his mother from the servitude of the Nagas.

Garuda soared from the mountain peak, causing the heavens themselves to tremble. Ominous signs appeared—Indra's thunderbolt blazed, and fire-trailing meteors plummeted from the sky. The very weapons of the gods turned against one another, an event unseen even in the great wars between the Devas and Asuras. Winds howled, meteors rained, and the sky roared with thunder, while the garlands of the gods withered and the sky's luminaries dimmed.

In great alarm, Indra turned to Brhaspati and asked:

"Why do such dire portents arise? I see no enemy capable of such a feat."

Brhaspati responded gravely:

"It is due to your own neglect, O Lord of the Devas. The Valakhilya sages, angered by your arrogance, have through their austerities brought forth a mighty being—a bird, son of Kasyapa and Vinata, of boundless power. He comes to take the Soma, and no force may stop him."

Upon hearing this, Indra warned the celestial guardians, who prepared for battle, adorning themselves with golden armor and wielding weapons gleaming with divine fire. The gods, their hearts set upon defending the elixir, arrayed themselves in an unbreakable brigade.

Meanwhile, Garuda reached the celestial realm, his mere presence sending shivers through the ranks of the gods. As he flapped his immense wings, he conjured a storm of dust that blinded and disoriented the guardians. The gods, unable to see their foe, attacked each other in confusion. Then, through the veil of chaos, Visvakarman, the divine architect and guardian of Soma, stepped forth to challenge Garuda. A great battle ensued, but the mighty bird struck him down with beak, wings, and talons, scattering the gods in disarray.

Indra commanded the Wind God to disperse the dust storm, revealing Garuda still standing strong amidst the host of gods. The celestial warriors unleashed a barrage of weapons—tridents, spears, bludgeons, and disks—but none could pierce the invincible bird. With a thunderous screech, Garuda struck back, routing the gods and forcing them to flee in terror. Blood rained from the heavens as the gods scattered in every direction, powerless before his might.

Reaching the elixir's sanctum, Garuda encountered a whirling, razor-edged iron wheel that spun endlessly, barring his way. Undeterred, he timed his movement with the wheel's rotation and slipped through it unharmed. Behind the wheel stood two venomous serpents with eyes like blazing fire. Their gaze alone could incinerate any intruder, but Garuda cast dust into their eyes, blinding them, and swiftly tore them apart with his talons.

At last, he seized the Soma, shattering the divine wheel as he ascended. Carrying the elixir without consuming it, his radiance eclipsed even the sun. As he soared, he encountered Vishnu, who, pleased with his strength and resolve, granted him two boons—immortality without the need for the elixir and a place above Vishnu himself. In turn, Garuda offered Vishnu a boon, and Narayana chose the mighty bird as his mount, making Garuda his eternal companion.

As Garuda continued his flight, Indra, wielding his thunderbolt, confronted him. With a mighty strike, he unleashed his weapon upon Garuda. Though struck, the great bird remained unharmed, laughing as he shed a single feather, saying:

"I honour the sage whose bones forged your thunderbolt, and I honour you, Lord of the Devas. But know that your weapon does not harm me."

Astonished by Garuda's immense power, Indra sought friendship rather than enmity. He asked for the return of the elixir, fearing that its consumption by another would upset the cosmic order. Garuda, having never intended to drink it himself, agreed, saying:

"I shall place it down, and you may reclaim it."

Upon reaching the Nagas, Garuda placed the elixir on sacred kusha grass, instructing them to purify themselves before drinking. As they departed to bathe, Indra swiftly reclaimed the Soma, thwarting their desire for immortality. When the Nagas returned, they found only the sacred grass, licking it in frustration. This act, however, caused their tongues to split, marking them forever; and the darbha/kusha stalks became purifiers, as they had been in touch with the Soma.

With his mission fulfilled, Garuda liberated his mother, Vinata, from servitude and established himself as the eternal enemy of the serpents. From that day forward, he ruled the skies, a symbol of might, wisdom, and divine grace, soaring above all beings as the Indra of Birds.
