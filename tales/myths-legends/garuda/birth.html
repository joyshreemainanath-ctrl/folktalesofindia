The Birth of Garuda

Reading time: 4 to 5 min

Cycle: Garuda (The King of Birds) Source: Tale of Astika - Adi Parva, Mahabharata Theme: Destiny, Maternal Bond, Cosmic Birth

A mother's impatience leads to a curse, paving the way for the hatching of a cosmic power.

Tale of Astika - Adi Parva, Mahabharata

Long ago, in the age of the gods, there lived two beautiful sisters, Kadru and Vinata, daughters of Prajapati, blessed with extraordinary beauty and divine qualities. Both became the wives of the revered sage Kashyapa, who was the forefather of all living beings. He was pleased with the devotion of his two wives, Vinata and Kadru, and bestowed a boon upon each.

Overcome with joy, the sisters eagerly made their wishes. Kadru asked for a thousand serpent sons, each equal in splendour, while Vinata wished for two sons who would surpass Kadru’s offsprings in strength, brilliance, and might. Kashyapa granted their desires but told Vinata that she would bear one and a half sons. Content with her blessing, Vinata rejoiced in the thought of her future children, just as Kadru celebrated bearing a thousand sons of equal splendour. Before departing for the forest, Kashyapa instructed them to nurture their eggs with great care.

In time, Kadru laid a thousand eggs, while Vinata laid only two. Their devoted attendants carefully placed the eggs in warm vessels, ensuring they remained undisturbed for five hundred years. When the time came, Kadru’s eggs hatched, revealing her thousand powerful serpent sons. But Vinata’s eggs remained unchanged, filling her with impatience and despair.

Unable to wait any longer, Vinata cracked open one of her eggs, only to find her son half-formed—his upper body fully developed while his lower half remained stunted. Enraged at his premature emergence, he cursed his mother:

Because of your impatience, you have stunted my growth. As punishment, you shall become the slave of the very woman you sought to rival. Only your second son will be able to free you—if you allow him to be born in his own time. But if you tamper with his egg as well, you will doom him to the same fate as mine. Wait patiently for five hundred more years if you wish for a son of unparalleled strength.

With this curse, the half-formed son ascended to the heavens, becoming Aruna, the dawn that heralds the rising sun.

Obedient to the curse, Vinata waited, ensuring she did not repeat her mistake. In due time, her second egg hatched, giving birth to Garuda, the mighty king of birds, destined to prey upon serpents. The moment he emerged, he soared into the sky, his brilliance rivaling the sun itself. Hungry and powerful, Garuda took his place as the greatest of all birds, consuming the food ordained for him by the Creator.

One day, as time flowed on, and the sisters, Vinata and Kadru, gazed upon the celestial horse Uchchaihshravas, the divine steed that had risen from the churning of the cosmic ocean (Samudra Manthan). A dispute arose between them—Kadru claimed that the horse’s tail was black, while Vinata insisted it was pure white. To settle the matter, they made a wager: whoever was proven wrong would become the servant of the other.

Kadru, unwilling to lose, devised a trick. She commanded her serpent sons to coil themselves around Uchchaihshravas’s tail, darkening it with their presence. When the sisters next beheld the horse, its tail had turned black. Vinata, as per their wager, became Kadru’s slave, and forced into servitude to tend to her serpent nephews.

Thus began Vinata’s long years of humiliation, waiting for the day her son, the mighty Garuda, would rise and change her fate forever. Moreover, the fates of the Nagas and Garuda were set in motion, shaping an eternal enmity between eagles and serpents.
